OPERATOR SPLITTING, POLICY ITERATION, AND MACHINE LEARNING FOR STOCHASTIC OPTIMAL CONTROL 

ALAIN BENSOUSSAN вҲ—, THIEN P.B. NGUYEN вҲ—вҲ— , MINH-BINH TRAN вҲ—вҲ—вҲ— , SON N.T. TU вҖ 

ABSTRACT . We propose a splitting approach to solve the second-order HamiltonвҖ“Jacobi equation, reducing it to a heat step and a purely first-order step. The latter is implemented using a gradient value policy iteration algorithm, enabling efficient characteristic-based machine learning methods. We establish convergence rates for the splitting method. In particular, the LвҲһ error is bounded below by O(h) and above by O(h1/7 ) for Lipschitz initial data; this improves to O(h1/5 ) for semiconcave data and to O(h1/3 ) for C2 data. We also prove an upper L1 error estimate of order O(h1/2 ) in the periodic setting, where h is the splitting step. For the first-order step, we provide a weighted L2 error analysis that shows exponential convergence. Each iteration solves linear characteristic equations and learns the value function by minimizing a weighted value gradient loss. The approach yields stable and accurate numerical results.

Classical numerical strategies include successive approximation schemes [ 9 , 8, 10 ], which it-eratively linearize the nonlinear HJB into a sequence of generalized HJ equations, each paired with pointwise minimization of the Hamiltonian. We propose an algorithm to solve (1.1) based on an operator splitting scheme. In this context, operator splitting means separating the nonlinear effect in (1.1) . Operator splitting methods for nonlinear partial differential equations has been studied in the literature. In [ 57 ], such methods were analyzed for purely first-order equations, with convergence rates provided, see also [ 40 , 45 ]. An abstract convergence theory for general schemes was developed in [ 7 ]. Later, [ 6 ] studied a splitting method for nonlinear degenerate elliptic and parabolic equations arising in option pricing models. See also the related method of dimensional splitting in [58, 49]. 1.1. Approach and contributions. In this approach, we split the evolution into a pure heat step and a purely first-order HamiltonвҖ“Jacobi step. The heat step is computationally straightforward to implement, while the first-order step is solved using a valueвҖ“gradient (also known as PI-

О») policy iteration method, first introduced in [ 13 ] and later studied for the time-independent first-order case in [ 12 ]. This first-order step has the advantage of being easily implemented in parallel and exhibits exponential convergence of the policy iteration in a weighted L2 norm. Moreover, it can be implemented using a machine learning algorithm with fast convergence [ 12 , 13 ]. Each iteration solves linear equations along characteristics from sampled initial states. Training minimizes a convex combination of mean-squared errors for the value function and its gradient. The value function is approximated using nonparametric models (e.g., radial basis functions or neural networks), with gradients obtained via automatic differentiation. Our numerical experiments confirm the accuracy and stability of the proposed scheme. We also provide a rigorous error analysis of the algorithm. (ii) First, we provide a fairly complete error analysis of the splitting scheme.
